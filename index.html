I am an Assistant Professor in the Department of Humanities and Social Sciences at the Indian Institute of Technology Roorkee, and a Visiting Researcher at the University of Johannesburg. My research spans behavioural economics, health economics, and empirical policy evaluation.

I study how households and individuals make decisions under constraints — with particular attention to health insurance design, out-of-pocket pharmaceutical expenditure, maternal nutrition, climate-sensitive disease proliferation, dishonesty, and choice architecture. Methodologically, I draw on difference-in-differences, field and discrete choice experiments, and applied microeconometrics.

I am a Principal Investigator on two J-PAL South Asia-funded projects on climate and dengue risk mapping, and on behavioural dietary interventions for pregnant women in Karnataka.

Research

My research agenda sits at the intersection of behavioural economics, health economics, and empirical policy evaluation. I am particularly interested in how behavioural frictions and institutional design affect welfare-relevant decisions in health, insurance, and household consumption.

On the health side, I examine the causal effects of publicly financed health insurance expansion — especially pharmaceutical coverage — on out-of-pocket expenditure among near-poor and missing-middle households, using administrative data and difference-in-differences designs.

My behavioural work uses field experiments and discrete choice methods to study nudges in maternal nutrition, time preferences, and dishonesty in digital markets. A parallel strand investigates climate-induced disease risk, focusing on how shifting temperature and rainfall patterns expand dengue proliferation zones and shape risk perception and health-seeking behaviour in Kerala.

Working Papers & Projects

Working paper

Does integrating pharmaceutical coverage into publicly financed health insurance reduce out-of-pocket spending?

Difference-in-differences evidence from Kerala, India. The paper exploits a policy reform that expanded outpatient pharmaceutical coverage under a state-run insurance scheme to identify its effect on catastrophic health expenditure and near-poor household welfare.
